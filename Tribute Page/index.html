Mahatma Gandhi

1869-1948

About Mahatma Gandhi

Mohandas Karamchand Gandhi, also known as Mahatma Gandhi, was a political and spiritual leader in India who is widely regarded as the father of the Indian nation. He was born on October 2, 1869, in Porbandar, a coastal town in present-day Gujarat, India.

Gandhi studied law in London and then spent several years in South Africa, where he developed his philosophy of nonviolent resistance to injustice. He returned to India in 1915 and led the Indian National Congress in the struggle for Indian independence from British rule.

Gandhi's nonviolent methods, such as civil disobedience, strikes, and boycotts, inspired other movements for social and political change around the world. He was a prolific writer and a passionate advocate for peace, justice, and human rights.

Gandhi's Legacy

Gandhi's legacy lives on today, not only in India but around the world. His philosophy of nonviolence has inspired many social and political movements, including the civil rights movement in the United States, the anti-apartheid movement in South Africa, and the pro-democracy movement in Myanmar.

Gandhi's writings, including his autobiography, "The Story of My Experiments with Truth," and his book, "Hind Swaraj," continue to be read and studied by scholars and activists. His life and teachings continue to inspire people of all ages and backgrounds to work for a better world.
